Allama Muhammad Iqbal
شاعرِ مشرق

Allama Iqbal, a polymath who donned the hats of a poet, philosopher, and politician, left an indelible mark on South Asia. Born in 1877, his Urdu and Persian verses resonated with Muslims yearning for self-reliance and freedom. His iconic poems like "Saare Jahaan Se Achcha Hindustan Hamara" (Our India is the Best in the World) fueled the Pakistan Movement, while philosophical musings on "Khuddi" (The Self) urged spiritual awakening. A towering figure, Iqbal's legacy transcends borders; his words continue to inspire millions, finding expression in music, art, and even architecture.

One of Allama Iqbal's most iconic lines is:
خودی کو کر بلند اتنا کہ ہر تقدیر سے پہلے
خدا بندے سے خود پوچھے، بتا تیری رضا کیا ہے
This powerful expression encapsulates Iqbal's concept of "Khudi" or selfhood, emphasizing the importance of individual empowerment and self-realization. His poetry, woven with such thought-provoking lines, serves as a timeless source of inspiration, encouraging people to strive for personal excellence and to navigate their destinies with a sense of purpose and conviction.

Beyond his literary and philosophical contributions, Allama Iqbal was also a respected academic and political figure. He served as a professor of philosophy and practiced law, later entering the political arena to advocate for the rights and interests of Muslims in India. His tireless efforts to promote unity and justice, coupled with his poetic brilliance, have left an indelible mark on the cultural, intellectual, and political landscape of the subcontinent, solidifying Allama Iqbal's enduring legacy.
